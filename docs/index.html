What this is, and what it isn't

The point. First Bristol publishes its live vehicle data, and there is a public 95% punctuality target for the area, but there is no accessible, route-level public scorecard showing whether that target is being met. This site builds one from the operator's own open data.
What we claim
  • These figures are our independent measurement of on-time performance at timing points, computed from First's public BODS real-time feed against the published GTFS timetable.
  • We use the DfT statistical on-time definition: a stop counts as on-time if the bus is between 1 minute early and 5 min 59 s late.
  • The method, the code, and the limitations are all open. Anyone can reproduce or challenge them.
What we do not claim
  • We do not claim First is breaking the law or withholding required data. Operators are legally required to publish timetables, vehicle locations and fares, and First does. Performance figures are not a statutory publication requirement.
  • We do not claim these are the official figures. They are an independent estimate and will differ from any internal operator or regulator measurement, which use different sampling and gating.
  • Low-sample routes and partial collection days are not reliable on their own, and are flagged as such.
How it's measured

A collector polls First's public SIRI-VM feed continuously. Each vehicle is matched to a scheduled trip by route, direction and first-stop departure time. When a bus passes within 150 m of a timing point, we record its lateness against the timetable. Days are rolled up into the route and headline figures above.

The 150 m gate keeps only genuine approaches to timing points; readings further out are excluded from the headline figure. Full method and sources are in AUDIT_METHODOLOGY.md alongside this site.

Honest limitations
  • Coverage is partial. We only see vehicles broadcasting on the feed at the moment they pass a timing point. "Trips seen" vs scheduled trips is a coverage indicator, not a lost-mileage figure.
  • Early days mean small samples. Until full days accumulate, per-route counts are low and the headline can swing.
  • Feed gaps (GPS dropouts, missing journey refs) reduce what we can match. We log and exclude rather than guess.
